User-Centered Design

The same model can be a confusing demo or a tool someone loves. The difference is whether you designed it for a specific person.

What It Is

User-centered design means shifting your focus from "does the model work?" to "can a real person use this to accomplish something they care about?" A model with 95% accuracy is useless if the output says POSITIVE 0.9534 and the user has no idea what that means. A model with 80% accuracy in a well-designed interface that says "This review is positive — the customer seems happy with the service" is genuinely useful.

Product designers, UX researchers, and software engineers do usability testing constantly. They put their product in front of someone who didn't build it and watch what happens. The confusion points become the redesign checklist.

When You Use It

Use this method whenever you've built something and want to know if it actually works for someone who isn't you. This is especially important for your final project — the difference between a demo and a tool is whether a non-builder can use it successfully without explanation.

The Steps

  1. Name your user. Not "anyone" — one specific type of person. A restaurant owner reading reviews. A student checking whether their essay sounds AI-generated. A journalist scanning headlines. The more specific, the better your design decisions will be.
  2. Redesign the interface for that person. Same model, completely different framing:

    For nobody

    Title: "Sentiment Analysis"

    Output: POSITIVE 0.97

    For a restaurant owner

    Title: "Review Checker"

    Output: Happy customer — consider thanking them for the feedback

  3. Test with someone who didn't build it. Share the URL. Give zero explanation. Watch what they do:
    • Where do they hesitate?
    • What do they misunderstand?
    • What do they wish the output told them?
  4. Write three things you'd change based on what you observed. Every point of confusion is a design opportunity.
  5. Ask the harder question: Should this tool exist for this person? Is it helpful, or is it invasive? Would you want an AI doing this to your own work?

The test that matters: Can someone who doesn't know what "sentiment analysis" means use your Space successfully in under 30 seconds? If not, the problem isn't the model — it's the design. Chapter 8 covers the specific techniques: titles, examples, output formatting, error states.

What You'll Discover

The key insight is that output framing matters more than model accuracy for real-world use. A 95%-accurate model with a raw JSON output is less useful than an 80%-accurate model that formats its output as a clear, actionable sentence. The model is the engine, but the interface is the steering wheel. Nobody cares how good the engine is if they can't drive the car.

This is also the method where you confront an uncomfortable question: should every tool exist? An AI that analyzes someone's writing style could be a helpful editor or a surveillance tool. The same sentiment model that helps a restaurant owner can be used to monitor employees. Designing for a specific user forces you to think about who benefits and who might be harmed.
